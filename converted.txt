ABSTRACT
Random telegraph signals (RTSs) are specific time-fluctuating signal patterns marked by a series of distinctive
switching events between well-defined signal levels. These signals are ubiquitous in many electronic, chemical,
and biological devices and systems. Analyzing RTSs unveils associated system structures and internal operation
mechanisms, offering valuable insights into performance sensitivity. Therefore, accurate parameter quantification of RTSs is essential for understanding their origin and significance. While two-level RTS analysis is
straightforward, complications arise at multiple levels, especially with unwanted background fluctuations. To
address this challenge, we developed a novel denoising autoencoder model with U-Net and bidirectional long
short-term memory (DAE UBL) for denoising multi-level RTSs degraded by Gaussian white and pink noise.
DAE UBL extracts lower-dimensional latent features with its encoder and reconstructs denoised RTS with its
decoder. Trained and validated with large datasets of noisy multi-level RTSs, our DAE UBL demonstrates
superior and stable denoising performance compared to four classic models with lower average median root
mean squared errors by over 78% and 63% for all RTS data accompanying various strengths of white noise
and pink noise. Average median signal-to-noise ratios in the DAE UBL analysis are increased by over 65%
and 56% for the white noise and pink noise datasets. In the time domain, DAE UBL effectively suppresses
both local and global fluctuations, thereby successfully removing background noise. Our model exhibits robust
performance in denoising multi-level RTSs with strong pink noise. We envision that our DAE UBL will be an
attractive denoising methodology for the complex multi-level RTS analysis.

1. Introduction
Random telegraph signals (RTSs) refer to observable digitized switching events that occur randomly between two fixed levels (Yuzhelevski
et al., 2000). These telegraphic signals are notably prominent and
directly related to performance sensitivity and reliability in various
places such as nanoscale electronic devices (Yang et al., 2022; Hung
et al., 1990), quantum devices (Kenfack et al., 2017; Kleinherbers
et al., 2022), photodetector image sensors (Lucas et al., 2022), and
biological systems of human breast cancer cells (Ribeiro et al., 2020).
The simplest form of RTSs involves two specific levels, where rapid
stochastic jumps happen between low and high levels separated by the
transition amplitude 𝛥RTS . In the time domain, between the jumps, 𝜏high
and 𝜏low are defined to represent individual dwell times at the high level
and low level, respectively. Fig. 1 presents a part of two-level RTS trace

in time domain with three RTS parameters. For the whole time trace,
the histograms of 𝜏high and 𝜏low follow a Poisson distribution, yielding
the average values of two-time constants denoted as 𝜏̄high and 𝜏̄low as a
manifestation of a memoryless property (Theodorsen et al., 2017).
Complex RTSs in real systems emerge from random switching
among more than two distinct levels, which indicates that there are
several two-level entities inside the systems (Goiffon et al., 2009;
Nagumo et al., 2010a; Awano et al., 2013). When multiple entities
may exist mutually exclusive, the RTS is referred to as normal RTS
(nRTS), resulting from the linear superposition of each two-level RTS,
which is characterized by its own parameters of 𝛥RTS , 𝜏̄high and 𝜏̄low .
However, if any correlations among the levels in different entities may
govern the complex dynamics, the resulting RTSs called anomalous
RTS (aRTS) become rather complicated. It is required to bring physical

Moreover, actual signals in experiments are mixed with these RTSs
and other background fluctuations, and some of which are modeled as
Gaussian white noise (Hida et al., 2013; Hendrickson, 2019; Majumder,
2011) and pink noise (Kogan, 1996; Bae et al., 2009; Ralls et al., 1984),
depending on its spectral behavior. If the unwanted noise amplitude
is smaller than that of RTS, classic denoising methods such as moving
average (MA) can differentiate RTS from the raw signals relatively well.
However, the background noise is noticeably stronger and a pink noise
is present, the analysis of both two-level and multi-level RTSs becomes
formidable, where a robust denoising technique is highly desirable.
RTS reconstruction is the process of restoring a pure RTS from
a raw noisy signal which combines the RTS with background noise.
Various efforts have been made to mitigate the interference of background noise through the development of RTS reconstruction protocols.
In 2009, Goiffon et al. introduced a reconstruction method based
on convolutional filtering to detect and reconstruct multi-level RTSs
in complementary metal-oxide semiconductor (CMOS) image sensors.
They were able to obtain the distribution of maximum amplitude and
the number of RTS levels from many pixels by reconstructing RTSs in
the presence of comparatively weak white noise level only (Goiffon
et al., 2009). Another approach is done in the frequency domain with
a discrete wavelet transform (DWT) technique followed by a threshold
extraction step on noisy RTSs from CMOS image sensors to reconstruct
only one pure two-level RTS out of strong background white noise
level (Hendrickson et al., 2020).
Recent advancements in machine learning have given rise to several novel methods of RTS reconstruction. The hidden Markov model
(HMM), a typical machine learning model, was applied to analyze noisy
two-level RTSs by generating two matrices with rank 2 that represent
the transition probabilities of two states (Li et al., 2013; Rabiner, 1989).

Data-driven models are aimed at finding connections between the

On the other hand, autoencoder (AE) (Leudar, 1989) based on deep
learning (leCun Yann et al., 2015) was employed to overcome the
aforementioned limitation by learning weights for denoising specific
parameter settings of signals. AE consists of two parts: an encoder,
which encodes the input data into a compressed latent space representation, and a decoder, which reconstructs the desired output result from
the latent space representation. The denoising autoencoder (DAE) is a
specific AE structure designed for signal denoising (Chiang et al., 2019;
Grozdić et al., 2017; Fan et al., 2020). Hendrickson et al. introduced a
DAE for dark current RTS denoising (Hendrickson, 2019). The model
can automatically denoise RTSs with different amplitudes and level
dwell times by training 90,000 RTSs, where its proven effectiveness has
been limited to the extraction for a computationally less challenging
class of two-level RTSs with white noise levels smaller than 40%.
To the best of our knowledge, existing RTS denoising models lack
the capabilities to effectively handle multi-level RTSs hampered by
either strong white or pink noise as background, which makes the
extraction of 𝛥RTS and 𝜏 much more challenging. The detailed discussions are given in Section 2.2. Furthermore, there is no attempt to
build an all-in-one model to handle many RTS types at once. Hence,
we have developed a novel DAE that is adaptable to a variety of
multi-level RTS classes across a wide spectrum of background noise
strengths, demonstrating superior performance in complex RTS analysis. The ultimate target is to apply our denoising model to real RTSs
across various fields. We envision that our denoising model can serve
as an end-to-end preprocessing method for analyzing complex multilevel RTSs in many applications, thereby advancing the development
of nanoscale electronic devices, quantum computers, biosensors, and
random number generators with high sensitivity. Section 2 explicitly
introduces various types of RTSs and outlines the process of generating
extensive datasets of RTSs for training and validating our DAE models,
which are comprehensively explained in Section 3. The details of DAE
architectures are elaborated upon two schemes of U-Net and bidirectional long short-term memory (Bi-LSTM). To quantify the performance
of our DAE model, we generate large datasets to cover six distinct
classes of RTSs with two background types of white noise and pink
noise for training and validation, and their ground truth values are
used to determine errors and the performance analysis is thoroughly
discussed in Section 4. Finally, we will conclude in Section 5 by
summarizing our work and discussing prospective work.

2.1. Classification of RTSs
In small-channel electronic transistor devices, a charge trap near
channel-oxide interfaces may capture or release a single electron or a
hole. The presence and the absence of a charge in the trap can determine two distinctive current levels. Often, multiple traps can exist with
their unique transition amplitudes. A simple case is when these traps
are mutually independent to contribute to the current values in time.
The resulting RTSs belong to nRTS (Wang, 1993; Yuzhelevski et al.,
2000; Simoen and Claeys, 2016; Guo et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018;
Yang et al., 2022; Robitaille et al., 2023). Fig. 2 shows representative
signal traces in time (left subpanel) and probability density functions
(PDFs) (right subpanel) of nRTSs from 1 trap (Fig. 2(a)), 2 traps
(Fig. 2(b)), and 3 traps (Fig. 2(c)), where the impact of background
noise in terms of the amplitude and the spectral behavior is readily
observed. The definition of 𝑄wn and 𝑄pn is given in Eq. (3). White noise
background simply adds the local regional fluctuations (left column),
whereas the pink noise as one of the low-frequency noise appends
not only local fluctuating amplitudes but also a low-frequency global
trend over the wide time range which is seen in the right column of
Fig. 2. For mutually exclusive nRTSs with many traps, the maximum
number of distinct signal levels 𝑁levels to occur rapid switching events
is theoretically calculated as,

On the other hand, aRTSs which go beyond the simple nRTS classification, originate from complex internal mechanisms among multitraps. Three categories of aRTSs are identified in short-channel metal–
oxide–semiconductor field-effect transistors (Guo et al., 2017; Zhang

which is a CNN developed for processing biomedical images (Ronneberger et al., 2015). A basic unit of the U-Net consists of a contracting
path and an expansive path; (2) a Bi-LSTM, which is a special kind
of RNN to exploiting long-term dependencies (Schuster and Paliwal,
1997). The resulting DAE model with both the U-Net and the BiLSTM is called as DAE UBL, whose architecture is depicted in Fig. 6,
containing unit layers of 1D convolution kernel (Conv1D()), maxpooling (MaxPooling1D()), and transposed convolution (Conv1DTranspose()).
The kernel size of all convolution and transposed convolution layers is

different input sizes of the conv1D layer. At the end of the encoding
stage, we add Bi-LSTM units with the forward and backward LSTM

concat(conv1, conv6) → Conv1D(1). The operation of concat() denotes
the concatenation between the same-size convolution outputs from
the encoder and the decoder. The introduction of concatenations is
skip connections adapted from the U-Net, a classic fully convolutional
network (FCN). Skip connections allow the DAE UBL to reuse low-level
features extracted by the encoder to prevent overfitting. The stride of
Conv1DTranspose is 2. After decoding, the output of Bi-LSTM layers is
remapped to a denoised signal with 𝑁= 4000. The optimizer of DAE
UBL is Adam, following the parameter setting from Kingma and Ba
(2014). Activation functions of convolution layers are rectified linear
units (ReLU) (Agarap, 2018) except the final convolution layer which
uses a linear activation function. Bi-LSTM layers leverage Tanh as
activation functions. Finally, MSE is estimated as reconstruction loss
of DAE UBL according to Eq. (7).

3.3. Denoising process with the DAE UBL
Fig. 7 and the pseudocodes of Algorithms 1 and 2 encapsulate the
denoising process flow (training and prediction) in the DAE UBL model,
which undergoes training with an extensive dataset of complex multilevel RTSs in various types. According to Fig. 7, this unified model is
used to predict an original RTS (green at the output) from a noisy RTS
(gray in the input). To the best of our knowledge, most RTS denoising
models in previous research works usually focus on a single RTS class
with weak white noise background. One of our primary objectives is to
establish an RTS denoising model which can handle a wide spectrum of
complex multi-level RTS classes. To achieve this, six RTS classes with
white noise or pink noise, as discussed in Section 2, are utilized to train
a single DAE UBL. During the training, all noisy RTSs maintain the same
signal length, aligning with the input length of DAE UBL, which can
facilitate uniform and effective training across different RTS classes.
At the prediction stage illustrated in Algorithm 2 and Fig. 7, if
the signal length of the noisy RTS to be predicted is longer than the
input length of DAE UBL (𝑁 = 4000), the noisy RTS is divided into
𝑛 segments, whose length matches to be 4000 so that DAE UBL can
predict segments directly. The trained DAE UBL then denoises each
segment sequentially, yielding a denoised signal per segment. These
denoised segments are then concatenated to produce the output, that
maintains the same signal length as the original noisy RTS. In the
event that the segment length of the 𝑛th segment is smaller than 4000,
the segment is formed by the last 4000 signal steps to perform the
denoising and prediction. The part that overlaps with the 𝑛th segment
is extracted as the last segment, ensuring a coherent and consistent

4.3.1. Computation time
Our DAE UBL approach is inherently different in the RTS analysis
compared to classic denoising models. First, it designs and builds a NN
architecture, which needs to be trained with various types of data sets.
The training time of Table 1 is 6.88 h using an A100 GPU and an AMD
Rome 7532 CPU, which means that our model complexity is moderate.
We further assess the prediction time to process each data set with

need to change any fitting parameters to complicated multi-level RTSs,

slow background fluctuations, which hinder reliable RTS extraction.

witness two major advantages of DAE UBL over MA. The first benefit of

DAE UBL compared to MA is that it can effectively mitigate unpatterned
background fluctuations composed of local regional, and global trend
fluctuations, consistent with the results of the synthetic RTS visual
evaluation. This advantage is particularly obvious in both examples in
Fig. 18, which reveal that DAE UBL almost erases the global trend of
pink noise with no level shift compared to MA. The second advantage

the trap is comparatively large. Unlike MA, the amplitudes of aRTS are
reliably detected in Fig. 18(b).
We conclude that the denoising outcomes of DAE UBL in both
real and synthetic RTSs excel that of MA, particularly for RTSs with
pink noise. This enhanced performance is instrumental in making the
reconstruction of RTSs with pink noise feasible.

5. Conclusions and future work
Recently, DAEs have emerged as highly effective tools for addressing one-dimensional signal denoising challenges, leveraging their robust feature extraction and reconstruction capabilities. We apply this
successful DAE scheme to the denoising task of complex multi-level
RTSs. Our model DAE UBL adopts the classic encoder–decoder structure, excelling in the feature extraction from noisy signals and the
subsequent reconstruction of denoised signals. Two key enhancements
to characterize our DAE UBL performance come from: (i) Bi-LSTM
neural networks that can capture past and future information; (ii) skip
connections from U-Net that utilize the reuse of low-level features to
minimize level shifts as a source of errors. These enhancements enable
